Foundations and Motivation for Transformers

The session began with a discussion of why foundational machine learning concepts remain important in the era of large language models. The instructor explained that understanding basic machine learning models and neural networks is necessary for understanding transformer architectures and making informed decisions about when simpler models may be more appropriate than large language models.

Key concepts reviewed included:

  • Traditional machine learning models versus neural networks.
  • The importance of understanding model trade-offs.
  • Why not every problem requires a frontier-scale language model.
  • The role of judgment in selecting the appropriate modeling approach.

Historical Evolution of Language Modeling

A historical overview traced the progression of language-processing techniques leading to transformers.

Evolution Timeline

flowchart LR A[N-gram Models] -->|Limited by vocabulary explosion| B[RNNs] B -->|Improved with memory cells| C[LSTM / GRU] C -->|Still sequential & memory fades| D[Seq2Seq with Attention] D -->|Removed recurrence| E[Transformers]

Topics discussed:

  • N-gram models and their reliance on word frequency counts.
  • Limitations of n-grams due to exploding vocabulary combinations.
  • Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their ability to process sequences while maintaining memory.
  • LSTM and GRU architectures as improvements over basic RNNs.
  • The challenges of sequential processing and fading memory in long sequences.
  • Sequence-to-sequence models with attention, which improved handling of long-range dependencies.

Participants discussed how attention mechanisms improved language understanding before the introduction of transformers.

The Transformer Revolution

The session introduced the central idea behind transformers: retaining attention while removing recurrent processing.

Major concepts included:

  • The significance of the “Attention Is All You Need” architecture.
  • Processing entire sequences in parallel rather than token by token.
  • Direct connections between distant words through attention.
  • Improved scalability with larger datasets and more compute resources.
  • Why transformers became the foundation for modern language models.

Transformer Family Split

flowchart TD T[Transformer Building Blocks] --> ENC[Encoder-only e.g. BERT] T --> DEC[Decoder-only e.g. GPT]

The discussion also briefly introduced:

  • Encoder-only architectures such as BERT.
  • Decoder-only architectures such as GPT.
  • The ability to derive different model types from the same transformer building blocks.

Transformer Architecture Overview

The instructor presented the high-level transformer pipeline and explained how language models process text.

Transformer Pipeline

flowchart LR A[Raw Text Input] --> B[Tokenization] B --> C[Embedding Generation] C --> D[Attention-based Contextualization] D --> E[Prediction of Next Token]

A central theme emphasized throughout the session was that language models fundamentally perform next-token prediction, with many advanced capabilities emerging from this simple objective.

Self-Supervised Learning and Cross-Entropy Loss

The session explained how language models learn without manually labeled datasets.

Main concepts:

  • Self-supervised learning through hidden or future tokens.
  • Creation of training targets directly from the text itself.
  • Reuse of cross-entropy loss from traditional classification problems.
  • Viewing next-token prediction as a classification task over an entire vocabulary.

The instructor walked through a detailed example showing how probability distributions, one-hot labels, and cross-entropy calculations relate to language-model training.

Tokenization

A major chapter of the session focused on tokenization.

Tokenization Strategies Compared

flowchart TD Tok[Tokenization Strategies] --> Word[Word-Level] Tok --> Char[Character-Level] Tok --> Sub[Subword] Word --> WordCon["Large vocabularies
Unknown words"] Char --> CharCon["No unknown words
Very long sequences"] Sub --> SubPro["Modern standard
Balances vocab size & sequence length
Handles rare words via fragments"]

Word-Level Tokenization

  • One token per word.
  • Suffers from large vocabularies and unknown words.

Character-Level Tokenization

  • One token per character.
  • Avoids unknown words but produces very long sequences.

Subword Tokenization

  • The modern standard approach.
  • Balances vocabulary size and sequence length.
  • Handles rare words by breaking them into reusable fragments.

Additional topics included:

  • Token IDs and vocabularies.
  • Special tokens marking sequence boundaries.
  • Why token counts affect latency and cost.
  • Language-specific tokenization differences.
  • The role of training data in tokenizer behavior.

Participants experimented with tokenization examples and discussed how tokenizers split different words.

Embeddings and Text Representation

The next section covered how tokens become meaningful numerical representations.

Representation Methods

flowchart LR A[Tokens] --> B[One-hot Encoding] A --> C[Bag-of-Words / TF-IDF] A --> D[Dense Learned Embeddings] D --> E[+ Positional Information] E --> F[Contextual Embeddings]

Representation methods discussed:

  • One-hot encoding.
  • Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF.
  • Dense learned embeddings.

Key ideas included:

  • Embeddings position semantically related concepts near each other in vector space.
  • Embeddings are learned automatically rather than manually designed.
  • Similar words and concepts tend to cluster together.
  • Positional information must be added because transformers do not inherently understand word order.

The session also compared static embeddings with contextual embeddings and discussed why contextual representations are more powerful.

Attention Mechanism

A major portion of the session focused on self-attention.

Self-Attention Flow

flowchart LR A[Input Token] --> Q[Query] A --> K[Key] A --> V[Value] Q --> S["Similarity via Dot Product (Q · K)"] K --> S S --> SM[Softmax Normalization] SM --> W[Weighted Aggregation of Values] V --> W W --> O[Contextualized Output]

Core concepts:

  • Query, Key, and Value representations.
  • Similarity computation through dot products.
  • Softmax normalization of attention scores.
  • Weighted aggregation of contextual information.

Examples illustrated how a token can gather information from all other tokens in the sequence and assign different importance levels to surrounding words.

Benefits discussed included:

  • Capturing long-range dependencies.
  • Avoiding the memory limitations of RNNs.
  • Enabling efficient parallel computation on GPUs.

Training Versus Inference

The session contrasted transformer training with inference.

Training vs Inference

flowchart LR subgraph Training T1[Entire Sequence] --> T2[Parallel Processing] T2 --> T3["Next-token Predictions at Every Position"] T3 --> T4[Cross-Entropy Loss] T4 --> T5[Gradient Updates] end subgraph Inference I1[Prompt] --> I2[Prefill Stage] I2 --> I3[Generate One Token] I3 --> I4[Append Token] I4 --> I3 end

Training

  • Entire sequences processed in parallel.
  • Next-token predictions generated at every position.
  • Cross-entropy loss computed over sequences.
  • Efficient gradient updates through parallel processing.

Inference

  • Prompt processed first through a prefill stage.
  • New tokens generated one at a time.
  • Each generated token is appended and reused for the next prediction.
  • Sequential decoding creates the primary runtime cost of language models.

KV Caching

The final technical topic covered inference optimization through Key-Value caching.

KV Cache Flow

flowchart LR A[New Token] --> B["Compute Key & Value"] B --> C[Store in KV Cache] C --> D["Reuse Cached Keys/Values for Attention"] D --> E[Generate Next Token] E --> A

Main points:

  • Recomputing attention for all previous tokens is expensive.
  • Keys and values can be stored after their first computation.
  • Cached values significantly improve generation efficiency.
  • Longer contexts require more memory for cache storage.
  • KV caching enables practical deployment of large language models.

Encoder and Decoder Architectures

The session concluded by comparing encoder and decoder approaches.

Encoder vs Decoder

flowchart TD Base[Same Transformer Foundation] --> Enc[Encoder Models] Base --> Dec[Decoder Models] Enc --> Enc1["Trained via masked-token prediction"] Enc --> Enc2["Bidirectional attention"] Enc --> Enc3["Understanding & representation tasks"] Enc --> Enc4["Classification & embeddings"] Dec --> Dec1["Trained via next-token prediction"] Dec --> Dec2["Attends only to previous tokens"] Dec --> Dec3["Designed for generation tasks"] Dec --> Dec4["Basis of modern LLMs"]

Encoder Models

  • Trained using masked-token prediction.
  • Can attend bidirectionally across text.
  • Strong at understanding and representation tasks.
  • Commonly used for classification and embeddings.

Decoder Models

  • Trained using next-token prediction.
  • Attend only to previous tokens.
  • Designed for generation tasks.
  • Form the basis of many modern large language models.

The instructor emphasized that both architectures are built from the same transformer foundation but are trained with different objectives, leading to different capabilities.

Putting It All Together: The GPT Architecture

GPT is a decoder-only transformer. It reuses every building block covered in this session, assembled into a single, repeatable pipeline that is trained once and then reused for both training and generation.

End-to-End GPT Flow

flowchart TD A[Raw Text Prompt] --> B[Tokenization] B --> C["Token Embeddings + Positional Embeddings"] C --> D["Transformer Block 1
(Masked Multi-Head Self-Attention + Add & Norm + Feed-Forward + Add & Norm)"] D --> E["Transformer Block 2"] E --> F["... N Stacked Blocks ..."] F --> G["Final Layer Norm"] G --> H["Linear Projection to Vocabulary Size"] H --> I["Softmax over Vocabulary"] I --> J["Predicted Next Token"] J -->|Append to sequence & repeat| B

Why It's "Decoder-Only" (Causal Masking)

flowchart LR T1["Token 1"] --> A1["Attends to: Token 1"] T2["Token 2"] --> A2["Attends to: Token 1, 2"] T3["Token 3"] --> A3["Attends to: Token 1, 2, 3"] T4["Token 4"] --> A4["Attends to: Token 1, 2, 3, 4"]

Unlike BERT's bidirectional attention, GPT masks out future tokens so each position can only see itself and everything before it — this causal constraint is what makes autoregressive, left-to-right generation possible.

Training GPT vs. Generating Text with GPT

flowchart TD subgraph Training["Training (one pass, parallel)"] TA["Full Text Sequence"] --> TB["Run Through All Transformer Blocks Once"] TB --> TC["Predict Next Token at Every Position Simultaneously"] TC --> TD["Compare to Actual Next Tokens via Cross-Entropy Loss"] TD --> TE["Backpropagate & Update Weights"] end subgraph Generation["Text Generation (autoregressive, sequential)"] GA["Prompt"] --> GB["Prefill: Compute & Cache K/V for Prompt Tokens"] GB --> GC["Predict Next Token"] GC --> GD["Append Token to Sequence"] GD --> GE["Reuse KV Cache + New Token"] GE --> GC end

How the pieces map to GPT specifically:

  • Tokenizer: Subword tokenization (e.g., byte-pair encoding) converts text into token IDs.
  • Embedding layer: Token embeddings + positional embeddings give each token a position-aware vector.
  • Stacked decoder blocks: Each block = causal multi-head self-attention → residual + layer norm → feed-forward network → residual + layer norm. GPT-family models simply stack many of these (more blocks/parameters = larger GPT variants).
  • Causal masking: Ensures token N only attends to tokens 1...N, enabling autoregressive generation.
  • Output head: Final hidden states are projected back to vocabulary size and passed through softmax to get next-token probabilities.
  • Training objective: Cross-entropy loss on next-token prediction, computed in parallel across the whole sequence.
  • Inference: Prefill the prompt once, then generate one token at a time, reusing the KV cache so each new token doesn't require recomputing attention over the entire history.

In short: GPT = tokenizer + embeddings → N stacked causal (masked) transformer decoder blocks → output projection to vocabulary — trained with next-token cross-entropy loss, and run at inference time autoregressively with KV caching for efficiency. Every concept from this session (tokenization, embeddings, self-attention, multi-head attention, transformer blocks, training vs. inference, KV caching) is a direct component of that pipeline.

Key Takeaways: What You Should Know After This Session

After completing Day 2, you should be able to confidently explain and reason about the following:

  • Judgment over hype: Not every problem needs a large language model — know when a traditional ML model or simpler neural network is the better trade-off.
  • Historical context: Trace the evolution from n-grams → RNNs → LSTM/GRU → seq2seq with attention → transformers, and explain why each step was needed (vocabulary explosion, fading memory, sequential bottlenecks).
  • Core transformer insight: Transformers keep attention but remove recurrence, enabling parallel processing and direct long-range token connections.
  • End-to-end pipeline: Describe the flow from raw text → tokenization → embeddings → attention-based contextualization → next-token prediction.
  • Self-supervised learning: Explain how models create their own labels from raw text and why cross-entropy loss (a classification loss) applies to next-token prediction over the vocabulary.
  • Tokenization trade-offs: Compare word-level, character-level, and subword tokenization, and explain why subword tokenization is the modern standard.
  • Embeddings: Explain how tokens become dense vectors, why embeddings cluster semantically similar concepts, and why positional information must be added explicitly.
  • Self-attention mechanics: Walk through Query/Key/Value, dot-product similarity, softmax normalization, and weighted aggregation — and why this captures long-range dependencies efficiently on GPUs.
  • Multi-head attention: Explain why multiple heads are used and how different heads emergently specialize (e.g., syntax, punctuation, context).
  • Transformer block anatomy: Identify all components — embeddings, positional encoding, multi-head attention, residual connections, layer norm, and feed-forward layers — and how stacking blocks builds model depth.
  • Training vs. inference: Contrast parallel training (whole sequence, cross-entropy loss) with sequential inference (prefill + one-token-at-a-time decoding), and why inference is the costlier runtime path.
  • KV caching: Explain why caching keys/values avoids redundant recomputation and why longer contexts increase memory requirements.
  • Encoder vs. decoder: Distinguish bidirectional, masked-token encoders (e.g., BERT, used for understanding/classification) from causal, next-token decoders (e.g., GPT, used for generation) — while recognizing both share the same transformer building blocks.
  • Practical skills: Comfortably tokenize text, generate embeddings, visualize attention, and compare transformer-based representations against TF-IDF for a downstream classification task, including the accuracy/latency/cost trade-offs involved.

Bottom line: Everything in modern transformer-based LLMs — from BERT to GPT — reduces to next-token (or masked-token) prediction, learned via self-attention over token embeddings, stacked into deep blocks, and optimized differently for training (parallel) versus inference (sequential, cache-assisted).
